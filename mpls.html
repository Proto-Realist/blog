MPLS Protocol

Multi-Protocol Label Switching (MPLS)

The Basics of MPLS

This is a protocol that operates at the network layer in the OSI model. Because it has attributes that resemble those of the Data Link layer, it is described as operating at layer 2.5 of the OSI Model. Essentially between the Data link and Network layers.

It is typically faster than using the standard internet protocols because of its use of pre-planned routes. These routes are called Label Switched Paths (LSP). Another unique characteristic of MPLS is the Label. While traditional protocols rely on IP addresses to route packets, MPLS uses labels. The functional difference lies in how they traverse the routers on their hops.

Train Ride Analogy

The typical internet protocol would be like a driver in a car trying to find their way through rush-hour traffic. This driver knows the destination address, but does not know how to get there. So driver, (packet) stops at every intersection (router) along the way to ask for directions. This is time consuming because a calculation needs to take place at every hop. Another downside is when the packet runs into a congested or broken path. It now needs to make the best decision of what is available to continue on its way.

Now, the MPLS packet is more like a passenger riding on a train. A real-world train follows tracks that are put into place long before the train or passenger starts their journey. This means that the engineers were able to plan out the most efficient routes to avoid damaged areas and congested areas. This makes for faster paths compared to last minute adjustments along the way. For unexpected obstacles, there are backup routes already assigned. The tracks in our analogy would be the Label Switched Paths (LSP) in a network. Just like train tracks are planned by Railway Engineers, the LSP is laid out by Network Engineers. The LSP is specific to each region where it has been set up. So every packet that is following this LSP takes the same route through that specific part of the network.

Now our analogous passenger has a ticket book to ride the train. This would be the Label on the MPLS packet. Now as the passenger (packet) hops from network to network, they show the ticket to the router. Then the passenger (packet) goes along for the ride without asking for any directions.

Advantages

The benefit to this is that it eliminates the need to calculate the next step at each hop, saving processing time. It also takes the most efficient route around congestion and obstacles instead of looking for the best way through the problem areas.

Name Break Down

Now, the meaning of the name Multi-Protocol Label Switching. 'Multi Protocol' simply means that it is capable of encapsulating many different types of protocols. The 'Label Switching' part requires some explanation. In our earlier analogy, the label was much like a ticket that allowed the packet to get onto the Label Switch Path (LSP).

Because of the vastness of the internet, there are many individual LSPs that a packet may need travel to get to its destination. Each LSP requires a distinct label for the packet to travel its path. So the switching part happens when the packet reaches the end of the LSP and needs to start on another. The old label is then 'switched' for a new one that matches the new LSP.
